The process for national infrastructure projects

National infrastructure projects are also called Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIP). The process involves lots of different people, including:

  • developers
  • members of the public
  • statutory bodies
  • charities
  • local authorities

A developer submits an application for a Development Consent Order (DCO) to the Planning Inspectorate. This can be developments like:

  • offshore wind farms
  • power stations
  • main roads
  • railways
  • utilities (new cables and water pipes)

The Planning Inspectorate is a government agency. We use the expertise of our teams and independent inspectors to decide if permission for a development should be granted. We then make a recommendation to the relevant Secretary of State, who make the final decision.

There are several steps in the process for DCOs.

Advice for people or organisations who want to have their say

There is a separate guide with more information about the steps in the process for people or organisations who want to have their say about a national infrastructure project.

  1. Step 1 Pre-application

    Pre-application is the first stage of the process. This is where the developer must run a consultation and engage with people and organisations in the area. They must also create detailed documents about the impact the project could have on the environment.

    Find out about what the developer needs to do and check our detailed guides

  2. Step 2 Review of the application

    This is called the Acceptance stage. This is when the developer sends us all of the information in their application including all of the consultation they have done. We look at all of the documents to check if we can accept the application for examination. We have 28 days to make this decision.

    How the review works and what happens next

  3. Step 3 Preparing for the Examination

    This is called the Pre-examination stage. We identify an inspector or a panel of inspectors called the Examining Authority and make a plan for the Examination stage. Anyone who wants to have their say will be able to register at this stage. Registration is open for 30 days.

    What happens during the preparation for the Examination

  4. Step 4 Examination of the application

    The examination stage is where the Examining Authority, the developer and anyone who has registered to have their say will get involved and comment on the proposed development at a number of set deadlines. This stage takes six months.

    What happens at the Examination of the application

  5. Step 5 Recommendation and decision

    Recommendation and decision is where the Examining Authority writes its recommendation report. This must be completed and sent to the Secretary of State within three months of the end of Examination. The relevant Secretary of State then makes the final decision.

    Making our recommendation and who makes the final decision

  6. Step 6 What happens after a decision is made

    Once a decision is made by the Secretary of State, there is a six week period where people can challenge the decision in the High Court. This is called a Judicial Review.

    What you can do after the decision has been made
