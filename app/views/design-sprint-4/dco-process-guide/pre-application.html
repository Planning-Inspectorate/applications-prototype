The Pre-application stage

Before a developer sends their application for a proposed development to the Planning Inspectorate, they must carry out public consultation. This feedback will then be taken into account and used to help shape the proposed project. This happens at the pre-application stage.

The developer must consult:

  • the general public
  • parish councils
  • statutory consultees
  • local authorities and councils
  • land owners and tenants

They must consider all comments and information from members of the public and organisations.

Getting involved in the developer's consultation during the pre-application stage is very important. This is your opportunity to put forward any questions and concerns, learn more about the development and how it might affect the area.

Check the guide on how you can have your say.

About the Pre-application service for developers

The Planning Inspectorate offers a pre-application service for developers who are preparing an application. This is a free service.

This includes:

  • providing advice about how to prepare an application, helping developers avoid extra cost
  • pre-submission checks and reviews of draft documents
  • checking developers have followed all the required steps

You can learn more about the pre-application service in the pre-application prospectus.

People or organisations who want to comment at the Pre-application stage

If you want to comment on a project at the Pre-application stage, you need to contact the developer. The developer must consult with the local community.

Getting involved in the developer's consultation during the Pre-application stage is very important. This is your opportunity to put forward any questions and concerns, and also find out more about the development and how it might impact the area.

There is a guide with more information about what people or organisations who want to have their say can do at each stage.

Part of The decision-making process for National Infrastructure Projects (NSIPs)

  1. Step 1 : Pre-application

    Pre-application is the first stage of the process. This is where the developer must run a consultation and engage with people and organisations in the area. They must also create detailed documents about the impact the project could have on the environment.

    Find out about what the developer needs to do and check our detailed guides

  2. Step 2 : Review of the application

    This is called the Acceptance stage. This is when the developer sends us all of the information in their application including all of the consultation they have done. We look at all of the documents to check if we can accept the application for examination. We have 28 days to make this decision.

    How the review works and what happens next

  3. Step 3 : Pre-examination

    We identify an inspector or a panel of inspectors called the Examining Authority and make a plan for the Examination stage. Anyone who wants to have their say will be able to register at this stage. Registration is open for 30 days.

    What happens during pre-examination

  4. Step 4 : Examination of the application

    The examination stage is where the Examining Authority, the developer and anyone who has registered to have their say will get involved and comment on the proposed development at a number of set deadlines. This stage takes six months.

    What happens at the Examination of the application

  5. Step 5 : Recommendation and decision

    Recommendation and decision is where the Examining Authority writes its recommendation report. This must be completed and sent to the Secretary of State within three months of the end of Examination. The relevant Secretary of State then makes the final decision.

    Making our recommendation and who makes the final decision

  6. Step 6 : What happens after a decision is made

    Once a decision is made by the Secretary of State, there is a six week period where people can challenge the decision in the High Court. This is called a Judicial Review.

    What you can do after the decision has been made
