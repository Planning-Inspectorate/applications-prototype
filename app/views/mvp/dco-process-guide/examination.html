About the Examination of the application

The Examination stage is where the Examining Authority will look at the proposed project and ask questions.

The developer, anyone who is registered to have their say, and anyone else involved in the project can comment on the proposed development or answer any of the questions at each deadline.

This is usually done in writing. The Examining Authority will create and publish a document of their questions. The Examination may also involve hearings if there are issues that need to be discussed in more detail.

This stage takes six months.

What happens at the Examination stage

The Examination stage is where the Examining Authority will look at the proposed development and ask questions.

The developer, anyone who is registered to have their say, and anyone else involved in the project can comment on the documents or answer any of the questions at each deadline.

You can send your comments by filling in the online form in the project section of this website.

If you have difficulty using online services, you can also send us information by email or post. You can check the project information in the project section of this website to find contact details for the Planning Inspectorate's case team.

What you can do if you have registered to have your say

Once you have registered to have your say, you must use your interested party reference when submitting comments.If you are registered, your comments must be taken into account by the Examining Authority.

You can:

  • comment on the proposed development at each deadline
  • attend any hearings

All project documents, comments from others who have registered and Examination questions will be published in the specific project section of this website.

To find out more about what you can do if you are registered to have your say, check our guide for having your say about a national infrastructure project.

If you have missed the deadline to register

You can still look at all of the project information and comment using the form, but there is no guarantee that your comments will be included in the Examination of the application.

You can contact the case team and ask to be registered late and given an interested party reference. If you choose to submit comments without being registered, the Examining Authority will decide if your comments can be taken into account.

Part of The decision-making process for national infrastructure projects

  1. Step 1 : Pre-application

    Pre-application is the first stage of the process. This is where the developer must run a consultation and engage with people and organisations in the area. They must also create detailed documents about the impact the project could have on the environment.

    Find out about what the developer needs to do and check our detailed guides

  2. Step 2 : Review of the application

    This is called the Acceptance stage. This is when the developer sends us all of the information in their application including all of the consultation they have done. We look at all of the documents to check if we can accept the application for examination. We have 28 days to make this decision.

    How the review works and what happens next

  3. Step 3 : Pre-examination

    We identify an inspector or a panel of inspectors called the Examining Authority and make a plan for the Examination stage. Anyone who wants to have their say will be able to register at this stage. Registration is open for 30 days.

    What happens during pre-examination

  4. Step 4 : Examination of the application

    The examination stage is where the Examining Authority, the developer and anyone who has registered to have their say will get involved and comment on the proposed development at a number of set deadlines. This stage takes six months.

    What happens at the Examination of the application

  5. Step 5 : Recommendation and decision

    Recommendation and decision is where the Examining Authority writes its recommendation report. This must be completed and sent to the Secretary of State within three months of the end of Examination. The relevant Secretary of State then makes the final decision.

    Making our recommendation and who makes the final decision

  6. Step 6 : What happens after a decision is made

    Once a decision is made by the Secretary of State, there is a six week period where people can challenge the decision in the High Court. This is called a Judicial Review.

    What you can do after the decision has been made
