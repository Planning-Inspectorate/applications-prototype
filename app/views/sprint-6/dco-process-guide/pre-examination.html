Pre-examination stage

The Pre-examination stage is where we prepare for an Examination. We will identify an inspector or a panel of inspectors called the Examining Authority and make a plan for the Examination stage.

This involves the Examining Authority deciding which issues will need to be discussed and what questions it might need to ask. The Examining Authority will also plan out what happens during the Examination, if it needs to arrange any hearings and each of the deadlines for comments.

Registering to have your say

You must register during the Pre-examination stage to make sure your views are included in the Examination of the application.

The registration for each project will be open for at least 30 days to give you time to register. The deadline for registering will be in the developer's advert, or you can check the project page.

There is a separate guide with more information about the steps in the process for people or organisations who want to have their say about a national infrastructure project.

The Preliminary Meeting

In the months after the registration for having your say closes, the Examining Authority will hold a Preliminary Meeting.

Several weeks before this meeting, a letter called the Rule 6 letter is sent to everyone who registered to have their say. This is to let everyone know when and where the meeting will be held. It will also include a draft timetable for the Examination of the application.

You cannot give your views about the proposed development at this meeting.

You can talk about

  • anything that may affect your ability to attend hearings or meet deadlines, for example other local events happening at the same time or issues with travel or working patterns
  • if there are any suitable local places to hold the hearings
  • if there are any groups of people who need a different approach to be able to take part in the process
  • any other matters relating to the draft Examination timetable

What happens next

The Planning Inspectorate will publish the meeting notes and a live stream recording on the project section of this website a week after the Preliminary Meeting.

A letter called the Rule 8 letter is sent to everyone who registered, as well as official bodies and people whose land is affected. This will have details of the Examination including the finalised timetable with all deadlines for Examination comments.

Part of The decision-making process for national infrastructure projects

  1. Step 1 : Pre-application

    Pre-application is the first stage of the process. This is where the developer must run a consultation and engage with people and organisations in the area. They must also create detailed documents about the impact the project could have on the environment.

    Find out about what the developer needs to do and check our detailed guides

  2. Step 2 : Review of the application

    This is called the Acceptance stage. This is when the developer sends us all of the information in their application including all of the consultation they have done. We look at all of the documents to check if we can accept the application for examination. We have 28 days to make this decision.

    How the review works and what happens next

  3. Step 3 : Pre-examination

    We identify an inspector or a panel of inspectors called the Examining Authority and make a plan for the Examination stage. Anyone who wants to have their say will be able to register at this stage. Registration is open for 30 days.

    What happens during pre-examination

  4. Step 4 : Examination of the application

    The examination stage is where the Examining Authority, the developer and anyone who has registered to have their say will get involved and comment on the proposed development at a number of set deadlines. This stage takes six months.

    What happens at the Examination of the application

  5. Step 5 : Recommendation and decision

    Recommendation and decision is where the Examining Authority writes its recommendation report. This must be completed and sent to the Secretary of State within three months of the end of Examination. The relevant Secretary of State then makes the final decision.

    Making our recommendation and who makes the final decision

  6. Step 6 : What happens after a decision is made

    Once a decision is made by the Secretary of State, there is a six week period where people can challenge the decision in the High Court. This is called a Judicial Review.

    What you can do after the decision has been made
